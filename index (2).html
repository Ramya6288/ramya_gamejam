Winning a game jam as a full-time professor and researcher taught me more about game development — and myself — than any lecture ever could. Here's exactly how it happened, and how you can do it too.

When people hear "Assistant Professor, Department of CSE," they don't usually picture someone huddled over a Unity project at midnight, obsessing over pixel art animations and game feel. But that's exactly where I found myself in the weeks leading up to Easter Game Jam 2024 — and I wouldn't trade it for anything.

I'm Dr. Puppala Ramya. I teach at Koneru Lakshmaiah Educational Foundation (KLEF) in Andhra Pradesh. I've spent 8+ years shaping future engineers, published over 20 research papers, and earned my PhD on deep learning and IoT systems. And I just won a game jam. Let me tell you how.

STEP 01

Think Small to Win Big

My first instinct, as an academic, was to go big. Pick a prestigious jam, assemble an ambitious scope, ship a polished product. That instinct was wrong.

Why I Targeted a Smaller Jam

Large game jams attract experienced full-time developers and established teams. They have hundreds of stunning submissions done within 48 hours. As someone balancing timetable duties, ERP responsibilities, and research publications, I had maybe a weekend's worth of focused dev time.

Easter Game Jam 2024 was the right size — organized, friendly, and with a community that genuinely valued creative effort over technical polish. I could compete here. And the weekend format forced me to cut scope ruthlessly, which turned out to be my superpower.

STEP 02

You Can't Win Without Finishing

My Secret Weapon: I Already Knew How to Teach Game Dev

Here's where my academic background actually became an advantage. I've been teaching Programming for Game Development using Unity at KLEF for years. I've earned both the Unity Associate Game Developer and Unity User Programmer certifications. I've led a full week-long Faculty Development Program on "Game Development in Unity" just months before the jam.

So while other participants were fighting with the engine, I already knew Unity's quirks cold. That saved me hours — hours I reinvested into art and game feel, which ended up winning me the #1 Art prize.

My Bottleneck Was Art — Until It Wasn't

In earlier projects I'd always underestimated art time. For this jam, I front-loaded the most critical visual assets first: the main character sprite, the core environment tiles, the UI. I built an "ambitious" scope and then assumed I'd only hit 60% of it — so I made sure that 60% was the game's heart.

An imperfect game that ships always beats a perfect game that doesn't.

STEP 03

Leverage Your Unique Background

As a researcher, I think in systems. My PhD on energy-efficient e-waste management using deep learning in IoT platforms trained me to optimize under constraints — exactly what game jams demand. Every mechanic I added had to justify its complexity cost.

My UX background (NASSCOM Adobe UX Foundation, Figma, Adobe XD) meant my menus, HUD, and tutorial flow were clean and readable from day one. Players could figure out the game without instructions. That UX clarity directly contributed to high ratings.

  • 🧩
    Use your existing knowledge base as a cheat code. Certifications in Unity, cloud, AI — they compound.
  • 🎨
    Readability beats beauty. Your art just needs to communicate clearly, not win a gallery show.
  • ⏱️
    Front-load the most critical 50% of your scope. That's what gets submitted and rated.
  • 📐
    Apply UX principles to game UI. Most jam games fail here — yours won't.
  • 🔁
    Enter small jams repeatedly. Each one teaches you your bottleneck. Remove it. Repeat.

THE RESEARCH ANGLE

What 20+ Papers Taught Me About Game Design

Writing research papers and shipping game jam entries have more in common than you'd think. Both demand a tight scope, a clear thesis, and ruthless editing. My published work on deep learning, IoT, sentiment analysis, and blockchain gave me a framework for thinking about systems — and every game is a system.

My paper on "Optimized Attention-based Bidirectional CNN-RNN for Facebook sentiment analysis" (accepted, Elsevier) required me to understand how humans respond emotionally to feedback signals. That's literally what game feel is. When does a player feel rewarded? When do they feel frustrated? Those are sentiment questions.

Key Publications That Informed My Design Thinking

Work published in Elsevier, Taylor & Francis, Springer, and IEEE conferences — on topics like e-waste IoT systems, machine vision, and blockchain trust models — all reinforced one skill: communicating complex systems simply. That's what a good game does.

TAKEAWAY

You Don't Need to Be a "Game Developer" to Win

I'm a professor. A researcher. A mentor to hundreds of engineering students. I run timetables, coordinate courses, host summits, and chase funding for conferences. And I won a game jam.

The skills you already have — whether that's UX, systems thinking, teaching, research, or just knowing how to deliver under a deadline — are your edge. Find the jam that fits your window. Ship something. Learn. Repeat.

The next jam I enter, I already know what I'm improving. That's the only system that matters.
